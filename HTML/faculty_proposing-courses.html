Proposing a UCORE course

Prospective UCORE courses must be approved through a formal proposal process.

Below is a list of the basic required information for UCORE course proposals. Instructors, please also note:

  • UCORE Handbook: consult the Handbook for details, including specifics about requirements for the particular course category (e.g., HUM, QUAN, CAPS) and for requirements for all UCORE courses.
  • Note: CAPS courses have additional requirements as outlined in the Handbook. In additon, resources for designing capstone assignments are available from ATL.
  • Sharepoint submission: the course proposal submission process is electronic through a UCORE SharePoint site. If you need access to the SharePoint site, please email Jeannie Holt at jmholt@wsu.edu.

Course Proposal Materials (updated 2013)

  1. A UCORE Course Proposal form with chair and dean’s signatures.
    (Please note: these are specially streamlined versions.)
    • Major curricular change form
    • Capstone curricular change form
  2. A supporting document of 150-250 words that contains a rationale for the request. The rationale should include how this course meets requirements for a UCORE designator, as specified in the UCORE Handbook.
    (Please note: Capstones have additional required elements.)
  3. A detailed course syllabus, which must include:
    1. Details specified in the “Guidelines for Syllabus Creation at WSU” (link), including the UCORE Category designator
    2. Detailed prompts for key graded assignments, especially writing assignments (e.g., For each major paper students must write, the contents and purpose of the paper will be specified in detail.)
    3. Statement of which learning goals — university learning goals, course-specific, and (if applicable) program-specific learning goals — the course will advance. To “advance” a student toward a learning goal means that by the end of the course, each student (who passes the course) will have a greater ability, as specified by the goal, than the student had when beginning the course. Students should have practice with feedback in order to improve their ability.
      1. Note: Do not choose too many learning goals for your course, or else the course may be spread too thinly. You must choose those learning goals assigned to the course (see Handbook for Required Contents for Specific Categories of UCORE courses), and you may choose some beyond that, but do not attempt all of them. Also make sure that the learning goals in the syllabus are consistent with the rationale, and that the UCORE learning goals are integrated into the activities of the course as listed in the syllabus, and not just cut and pasted into the syllabus.
